Arkansas attracts hundreds of roofing contractors after every major storm. Most are legitimate. Some are not. Here's the exact process for finding a trustworthy contractor — and the red flags that should end the conversation immediately.

Step 1: Verify Credentials Before the Conversation Goes Further

Before you spend time getting an estimate from any contractor, spend 60 seconds verifying their credentials. This step eliminates a significant portion of the bad actors before they ever get on your roof.

Arkansas Contractor License: Check at ALC.Arkansas.gov. Search by company name or individual name. A valid license means the contractor has passed Arkansas's competency requirements and carries the required bonding and insurance. No license number means no conversation. SMI Roofing is fully licensed — ask for our number and verify it.

General Liability Insurance: Request the certificate of insurance directly. Minimum $1 million coverage, preferably $2 million. A legitimate contractor provides this instantly because they carry it routinely. If they need to "get back to you" on insurance documentation, that hesitation is your answer.

Workers' Compensation Insurance: This one is separate from general liability and critically important. If a crew member is injured on your property and the contractor does not carry workers' comp, your homeowners liability policy may be exposed. Always request workers' comp documentation specifically — do not assume it is included with liability coverage.

Physical Local Address: Any contractor serious about their work in Arkansas has a real local office address — not a PO box, not an out-of-state corporate address. SMI is at 302 East Parkway Drive, Suite C, Russellville. You can walk in. Verify that any contractor you consider has a verifiable local presence before proceeding.

Step 2: Evaluate the Review Profile Carefully

Google reviews are the most reliable contractor reputation signal available, but reading them intelligently matters more than just checking the star average.

Volume matters more than most homeowners realize. The difference between a contractor with 12 reviews and one with 231 is not just quantity — it is the statistical reliability of the signal. Twelve reviews can easily reflect the contractor's friends and family. Two hundred thirty-one reviews from verified Google users who each took time to write reflects a consistent pattern of real customer experience.

Rating consistency and recency both matter. A 4.9 rating is excellent. A 4.2 rating in a market with multiple 4.8+ competitors should give you pause. More importantly, look at the distribution of recent reviews — a company with 150 positive reviews from three years ago and spotty recent activity tells a different story than one with consistent, recent, detailed reviews.

Read negative reviews and responses carefully. Every contractor of any size will have occasional unhappy customers. What matters is how the company responds. Professional, solution-focused responses that acknowledge the concern and describe remediation show operational maturity. Defensive, dismissive, or attacking responses to negative reviews are a character indicator that applies to how you will be treated if something goes wrong on your job.

Look for insurance claim references specifically. A reviewer who says "SMI handled the entire insurance process and I paid nothing out of pocket" is telling you something concrete that a generic five-star review does not. That insurance claim expertise is uncommon in the industry and represents a genuine capability gap between contractors.

Step 3: Get Three Estimates and Know What You're Comparing

Three estimates gives you market context and helps you identify outliers in both directions. But comparing estimates only by total price is a mistake — you must verify you are comparing the same scope.

When reviewing estimates side by side, confirm: the material brand and product line (GAF Timberline HDZ versus a builder-grade 3-tab shingle is a significant quality and warranty gap), the square count and waste factor (see our article on what roofing squares mean), warranty terms for workmanship (10 years versus 1 year is a real difference — get it in writing), and scope completeness (does the estimate include drip edge replacement, ice and water shield, synthetic underlayment, ridge cap, and debris removal, or are these left ambiguous?).

A significantly lower estimate almost always means one of three things: lower quality materials, reduced scope with components left out, or a contractor who will add charges mid-job when they discover scope items they did not include. None of these outcomes benefit you. The goal is not the lowest number — it is the best value for a complete, properly specified job.

Step 4: The 6 Questions to Ask Before Signing

Run every contractor through these six questions before committing. A legitimate contractor answers all of them without hesitation. Evasion, deflection, or irritation at being asked are disqualifying:

  • (1) What is your Arkansas contractor license number? Write it down. Verify it at ALC.Arkansas.gov before signing anything.
  • (2) Will you provide a certificate of insurance showing both general liability and workers' compensation? The answer should be "absolutely, give me one minute."
  • (3) What specific materials will you use — exact manufacturer, product line, and color? "Quality shingles" is not an answer. "GAF Timberline HDZ in Charcoal" is an answer.
  • (4) What does your workmanship warranty cover and for how long — in writing? SMI provides a 10-year workmanship warranty. Ask to see the warranty document, not just hear the number.
  • (5) Will you be present at my insurance adjuster meeting? A contractor with genuine insurance claim experience says yes immediately. One who has not done this before will hedge.
  • (6) Is there a written contract specifying start date, material specs, complete scope, and payment schedule? If the answer is anything other than yes, walk away.
