The Quick Answer

Most commercial flat roof leaks in Arkansas are repairable — the right fix depends on the membrane. TPO and EPDM seam failures are re-welded or patched, punctures are sealed, and aging roofs with widespread minor wear are often best restored with a roof coating. The faster you respond to a leak, the less interior and structural damage you face.

What Causes Commercial Flat Roof Leaks?

Flat and low-slope roofs fail in predictable places. The most common sources of leaks on Arkansas commercial roofs are:

  • Ponding water — standing water from poor drainage that breaks down the membrane over time
  • Seam separation — open or failing seams where membrane sheets join
  • Punctures — from foot traffic, dropped tools, or storm debris
  • Failed flashing — at penetrations, curbs, parapet walls, and rooftop units
  • Blisters and open laps — caused by trapped moisture or adhesion failure
  • Clogged drains and scuppers — that back water up under the membrane
  • Storm and hail damage — punctures and bruising from Arkansas storms

How Are TPO and EPDM Roofs Repaired?

The two most common single-ply membranes are repaired differently. On a TPO roof, seams and patches are heat-welded — a hot-air weld fuses new membrane to old, creating a monolithic, watertight bond. On an EPDM (rubber) roof, repairs use primer and splice tape or a cover patch to seal seams and punctures. In both cases, the first step is locating the true source of the leak, which often sits well away from where water appears inside the building.

How Are Modified Bitumen and Built-Up Roofs Repaired?

Modified bitumen and built-up (tar-and-gravel) roofs are repaired with compatible bituminous materials — torch-applied or cold-applied patches, re-flashing at penetrations, and mastic at seams and laps. These asphaltic systems are durable but need failing laps and flashing addressed before water tracks under the field. Whatever the system, SMI's commercial roof repair team matches the repair method to the existing membrane so the fix actually lasts.

When Is a Roof Coating the Right Repair?

When a flat roof is aging but structurally sound — widespread minor wear, surface cracking, chronic ponding, UV degradation — a fluid-applied roof coating is often the smartest, most economical fix. A silicone or acrylic coating seals the entire surface, stops minor leaks, reflects heat, and can extend the roof's life by 10 to 15 years for a fraction of replacement cost. The one hard rule: coatings work over dry roofs. If insulation under the membrane is saturated, a coating only traps the moisture. Our guide to commercial roof coatings in Arkansas covers when a coating works and when it does not.

How Should You Respond to a Commercial Roof Leak?

When water is coming in, move fast to limit the damage:

  • Protect the interior. Move or cover inventory, equipment, and electronics; place containers under active drips.
  • Document everything. Photograph the interior damage and the water's path — you will need it for insurance.
  • Mark the location. Note where water is entering so the crew can trace it to the source on the roof.
  • Call a commercial roofer for an emergency repair. A temporary patch stops the bleeding until a permanent repair can be scheduled.

Do not ignore a small leak. Trapped water spreads under the membrane, saturates insulation, feeds mold, and can compromise the deck — turning an inexpensive patch into a full tear-off.

Repair or Replace? When Flat Roof Repair Isn't Enough

Repair is the right call when the membrane is largely sound and the insulation is dry. Replacement becomes necessary when insulation is saturated across wide areas, leaks keep returning despite repairs, or damage covers a large share of the roof. An infrared scan or core sample tells you which. For the full decision framework, see our guide on when to replace vs. repair a commercial roof.
